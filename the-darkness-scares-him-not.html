The Darkness Scares Him Not


by Todd Crone


Charlie was my closest friend at EvilCorp.  He sat with me at lunch and listened to all my rants while nodding his support.  He nursed drinks as I rambled on about the injustices I endured on my projects.  He even tolerated my complaining in the break room as he sucked Dorrito (or Cheeto) dust off his fingers.  Charlie was a good work pal until the day of the big presentation.

“You ready for this man?”  Charlie said as he peeked over my cube wall.  He seemed bored.  I had no idea how he achieved that relaxed state at our office.  It seemed as though I was always chasing the next deadline, whereas Charlie never seemed to care that he had never met a deadline. Some days, I envied him.  Today was one of those days.

“Yeah,” I said as I worked on my last few slides.

“You sure you wanna do this?”  Charlie said with an oddly serious tone.  That was not his way.

“Yes,” I said.

“Ed’s not gonna like it,” he said.  He was right.

Fuck Ed, I thought.

“Maybe Ed should like it,” I said.

“Ed likes money,” Charlie said.  He was right again, and the suggestions in my presentation were certainly not going to make the company more money, at least not in the short term.

“I have to do this,” I said with a measure of resolve.

Charlie nodded as he always did and said, “Okay, I’m with you.”

He wasn’t.

#

With less than a minute until meeting time, I finished the last slide, closed my laptop, adjusted my glasses, looked at Charlie, and said, “Let’s do this.”

Charlie smiled back and offered me the hallway with a sweeping bow of mock deference.  My eyes rolled.  But I wouldn’t let him kill my enthusiasm.  I was doing something important.  I was taking my project—our company—to the next level.  Away from the narrow, focused mindset of the past.  Away from mediocrity.  I would show everyone how cutting corners to make an arbitrary deadline at the expense of quality was short-sighted.  And that finishing a project under budget means nothing if the result doesn’t delight our customers.

As I approached the conference room, I could see it was full of people.  At first, I was thrilled.  Then, I was nauseous.  I could feel the red heat rising on my face.  Cold sweat formed on my ears.  Why my ears?  Charlie looked back at me with big eyes and an uncomfortable smile.  This was bigger than we expected.

That was the moment my good friend Charlie failed me.  Reaching into his pocket, he pulled out his cell phone and quickly placed it up to his ear.  His face became a serious mask.  Looking back at me, he raised his index finger and mouthed, ‘One minute.’ 

Then he bailed on me.

#

As I crossed the threshold, I saw the room was filled beyond capacity.  The thralls from my company were out in force.  To most of them, this meeting was an hour they didn’t have to spend at their desks pretending to be productive.  They just wanted to get through the day.  Maintain the status quo.  Nothing I said would likely move them.  But I had to try.

Amongst them were the “meeting moths” that couldn’t help but be drawn to any conference room with three or more people.  They weren’t invited.  They didn’t even know what this meeting was about.  But you could be assured they would find some way to “contribute.”  These people were lost outside of a meeting, and they were the first to suggest a follow-up meeting.  The infamous meeting to discuss the meeting.

My favorite people were standing in the back.  Pete, Paul, and Tom.  They were each at the same level in the company as me and worked on similar projects.  If you run into these guys in the hallway, they act like your best friends.  They quickly commend you on your work with a “Good job, bud” or an “Awesome work, bro.”  But in the conference room—their arena—they are your enemy.  They will do anything to nominalize your work in front of as many people as possible.  It is always easier for them to pump themselves up by knocking down some of the competition.  And instead of offering suggestions privately BEFORE the big meeting so you can make adjustments and improve your presentation, they take mental notes on early drafts so they can act smart during the actual meeting and take you down a notch or two.  Yeah, these guys are the worst.

At this point, I realized all eyes were on me.

“Ok,” I said.  ”I’ve created this presentation to show how we can improve the quality of our current and future projects.  This will help us retain customers and gain new ones.  I hope you enjoy it.”

The first slide appeared on the screen, and the room faded away.  I fell into a rhythm.  A dreamlike state enveloped me.  Slides magically transitioned to the next and the next.  I wasn’t even aware I was holding the control in my hand.  I discussed adjustments to our process and cited studies that supported them.  We could improve the quality of our work.  It would take more time and cost more initially, but in the long run, our customers would appreciate our improvements, and we would grow our business through word of mouth and reputation more than aggressive marketing schemes.

Open faces nodded all around the room throughout the presentation.  Thirty-two minutes later, I was done, and relief coursed through my body.  Modest applause, and it is over.

“Any questions?” I said, hoping that I might get some good ones.

Tom’s hand raised.

Lovely.

“Yeah, Tom,” I said reluctantly, pointing to the back of the room.

“Has Ed seen any of this yet?” he said.

I hesitated.  I felt my face getting hot again.  This time, it wasn’t because of anxiety.  I took a few deep breaths to keep my cool.

“I haven’t spoken to him yet,” I said as groans erupted and frowns fell.

I lost them.

Tom smiled at Paul and Pete.  They all shook their heads in silent agreement.

The conference room started to clear as I collected my laptop and what was left of my dignity.  Paul came up to me smiling and faked a punch to the chest.  I don’t know why he always does that.  Did he box in college?  He’s always shadowboxing with Pete and Tom in the hallways between meetings.  Why is it my turn all of a sudden?  It’s pretty annoying, but I smile awkwardly.

“Great prez, bro!  Let us know when you’ve shown this to Ed.  We’d REALLY like to implement some of these things,” he said with a hint of victory, then headed towards the door with the rest of the mob.

Out in the hallway, Charlie was waiting for me.

“Good job, man,” he said.  ”I saw it from here.  I didn’t want to interrupt after you got going.  It looked like it went really well.  I think Ed might actually go for it.”

No, he won’t.

#

Back at my cube, Charlie came over and acted like nothing happened.

“Hey man, wanna go to that new Mexican place after work for some margaritas?” he said, then noticed I was packing my stuff into a pathetic little box.  “What are you doing?”

“I’m going to talk to Ed,” I said.

“Okay?” he said, hanging on that ‘ay’ for a bit as the confusion on his face intensified.  It’s a gift he has.  Blankness.  Another one I wish I had occasionally.  Now would be nice.

I glared at him, waiting for him to figure it out, then returned to packing a bobblehead barely holding together after being knocked off the shelf multiple times by errant Nerf shots from Charlie across the cube farm.

“When Ed rejects my ideas, I’m quitting,” I said, throwing the bobblehead in the trash.

The confusion left his face.  “Don’t you mean if?” he said.

“Sure, Charlie.  If,” I said, dropping a stapler into my pathetic little box.  After I tossed in a few more things, I grabbed the box with both hands and glared at him again, waiting for more insight.

He put his hands up in surrender and backed away.  With a brief frown, his eyes met mine, and I sensed the slightest look of disappointment.  Then he turned and made his way back to his cube.

#

Moments later, I stood on the precipice of Ed’s office.  The top dog.  The big kahuna.  The project manager.  I sat my pathetic little box beside the door, straightened my shirt, messed with my hair, and knocked.

“Come in,” he said from the other side.

As I opened the door, the smell of a Bahama breeze hit me squarely.  For some reason, his office always smelled better and looked cleaner than the rest of the office.  Even his bookcase looked dust-free; I’m confident this guy rarely reads.  The books most prominently displayed were “Leadership,” “The Effective Executive,” and my favorite, “The Art of War”.  All for show, of course.

He was on the phone, sporting a blank smile.  He gestured me in.  I couldn’t tell if the smile was for me, whoever was on the phone, or if he had just grinned his way through so much shit that this was the default expression frozen on his face.

“Okay, honey, I have something here I need to deal with.  I’ll call you later,” then he hung up with a beep.

He had something here he needed to deal with.  Lovely.

“I heard you made a big impression on everyone this afternoon,” he said, still smiling but eyes narrowing.  I could tell he was trying to figure out what I was after.  One of his spies had most certainly told him about the meeting, and he’d had time to mull it over.

“Yes, I think we can do better for our customers with some minor adjustments to our process,” I said, sticking to my guns.

He leaned back in his chair, hands behind his head.  His eyes dug into me, but for once, I didn’t feel the need to look away.  Instead, I stared right back through him.  I noticed he was sitting in a chair from the new conference room down on the third floor.  One of the chairs he specifically told the rest of us not to touch in a very condescending email.  The rest of us had old chairs that had seen better days.  They were likely brought over from the last company that our executives drove into the ground.

Leaning forward in his stolen chair, he placed his hands squarely on his cherry desk and said,” I can appreciate that.”  No, he couldn’t.  ”Unfortunately, we cannot afford the extra time or expense,” Another lie.  “You understand, right?”  His eyes stayed focused on me.  His smile was gone.

“No,” I said.

“No?  I’m sorry?” he said, a hint of a grin forming on his smug face.

“I said no, Ed.  I’m not going to understand.”  He squinted and prepared himself to interrupt me, so I didn’t let him.  ”I’m also not going to get myself worked up trying to rehash something I already covered in a detailed presentation that you couldn’t be bothered to walk down the hall and stick your head in to see,” I hesitated towards the end.  I wanted to say “big FAT head,” but I caught myself.

“Well, then I’m not sure where we are then,” Ed said.  His shoulders crept forward.  He was preparing for me to say something he could use later to discredit me and everything I’ve tried to communicate today.

“I quit,” I said, realizing the longer I stay in Ed’s office, the more likely I will chuck one of those management books that he has never read out the window.  I gathered myself and headed for the door.

“You realize if you quit on me, I’ll ensure your career is dead?” he said, letting the last word hang in the air.

I paused briefly.  Then, I continued out the door, picked up my pathetic little box, and headed out.

A few moments later, I stood before the elevators, clinging to my pathetic little box, and considered heading over to Charlie’s cube and saying goodbye when Pete walked up.  He took a bite of an apple I’m confident he stole from the third-floor conference room.  The fruit basket down there got replenished each week, but everyone knew the fruit was for prospective clients, not employees.  Another fact that Ed had made abundantly clear in a managerial email to the rest of us serfs.

“You gotta push the button, bro,” Pete said, leaning around me, pushing the down arrow, and giving me a healthy clap on the back.  I fought the urge to say something back, and Pete continued down the hallway, tossing the partially eaten, stolen apple in the trash can along the way.


#

At home, I went through the typical ritual.  I threw my mail (mostly bills, junk mail, and stuff addressed to some dude no one in the apartment complex seemed to remember) on the kitchen table.  I checked the fridge for food and found none (at least nothing safe to eat anymore).  Then I grabbed a beer, sat on my lumpy couch, turned on the TV, and looked for something to watch for a few hours until hunger forced me to order some takeout.

As I scrolled through available shows, I noticed some papers poking out of another pathetic little box that I must have shoved under my entertainment center shortly after I moved in.  I pulled it out and saw it was full of crap my best friend back home gave me before I moved away to that city with that job that would “change everything.”  In the box, he’d dumped all sorts of things I’d left at his place over the years.

Amongst the high school yearbook, an old deck of cards, a map of medieval Europe, and a Harry Potter book (never read it), I found a short story I wrote in middle school wrapped in black construction paper.  The first page has a crude colored pencil drawing of a cloaked, mounted figure on a green ridge with trees going down the side and a two-dimensional sun.

I read the title and smiled.

The Darkness Scares Him Not…

Just holding it, I remembered the pure joy I had writing it.  Streaming services be damned.  This deserved a read.

#

A lone cloaked figure coaxed his chestnut steed across the deep valley.  The ground was still damp from Spring’s first rain, and the last remnants of Winter’s cold grasp lingered.  The sun was high, but the rider knew he would not find his quarry until the sun cowered beyond the horizon.  There was little point in making haste. The night would come.  Darkness would fall.  Evil would have its time.  He rode onward.

People of this land had come to know this rider not by name or face but as the light in the darkness.  He struck fear in the evil he hunted, as his reputation was not based on the swiftness of his sword or the power of his spells.  He was not the best warrior in the land.  He was not a wizard who evoked powerful magicks.  He owned no castles housing mighty warriors.  Instead, his reputation was based on his bravery in the face of evil.  When others ran to hide behind stone walls, he continued forward.

Although he feared the darkness, as any mortal would, he did not allow it to scare him.  As wise ones have said, bravery is not the absence of fear but conquering it in the moment.  Many faded before him when they could not wield fear against him.  This evil he pursued now had not faded.  It waited.

Dark clouds formed above as if Mother Nature knew what lay ahead.  And as evening approached, rain began to fall.  At first, the raindrops were gentle, a polite warning, but they grew steadily stronger until they fell as hard stones.  They beat on the rider as lightning played across the sky and angry gods roared thunder.  A warning not heeded.

Then, the storm subsided as quickly as it began, and the rider found himself on top of a ridge overlooking another valley.  This one housed evil.  Ash was in the air.  Death was in the cold breeze that greeted them.  Jagged, dangerous, dark figures replaced daytime shadows.  Moon’s children threatened to pull the rider from his horse and slay him.  He did not hesitate.

Urging his mount down into the dark, wooded valley, branchy fingers tore at his cloak and face.  Sharp rocks bit his horse’s hooves, causing it to stumble.  Deeper they went until the trees gave way to a clearing.  The wind died in a whisper, and the night was still.  His long blade hissed as he released it from its sheath.  He sensed evil.  His mount shifted, shook its mane, and chomped at its bit in protest.  The rider did not listen.

Pounding hooves broke the silence as mounted shadows erupted from the darkness across the clearing.  A trio of evil blades burned bright in the night sky.  The lone warrior’s steed bucked in fear.  It hadn’t the bravery to meet this charge.  Despite the long warrior’s efforts to calm it, his mount threw him to the ground and bolted to safety.  The warrior had only just recovered when they were upon him.  He swung his sword with all his strength, but defeat awaited him.  These shadows had already met death before, and only due to dark magic did they live again.  The lone warrior’s sword could not damage them as it lacked the enchantments to harm the undying.  Soon, the futility weakened him, and his defenses failed.  The blades from the undying burned his face, pounded his chest, and stung his back.  As the lone warrior fell to his knees, the undying snatched him up and dragged him away.

They brought him before a great throne made from dark brimstone glowing with the heat of the abyss.  Upon it sat an immense being of skin and scale.  Batwings loomed behind him as his eyes showed with the cruel glow of hate’s embers.

“What a great hero you are,” it said with a vast predator’s smile.

His undying laughed as demonic hyenas.

“Bow down to me and join my army as others have,” the demon said, offering his undying, “and you will never know defeat.  Do not, and you will die after suffering pain as you have never experienced.”

The lone warrior, on the edge of death, the eternal darkness, pulled himself to his feet.

Looking through the demon, he said, ”The darkness scares me not.  You may take my life, but never enslave my soul.”  The dying man’s spirit stepped from his body and floated away.

#

After I closed the black construction paper cover, I realized I would be okay.  Even in middle school, I understood something about doing the right thing despite the consequences.  If that younger version of me could write about courage, I could have a little.

The phone rang as I sat in that particular moment alone in my apartment.  It was Charlie.

“Hey man, what’s up?” I said.

After a brief moment, he said,” I’m sorry.”

“It’s okay, man. I—”

“No,” he said,” It wasn’t okay.  I should have backed you up.  I failed you.”

I felt a pit in my stomach and said,” No worries.  You’ve been there for me all along.  It wasn’t fair for me to give you attitude.”

“You got any idea what you’re gonna do?” he said, forgiving me instantly.

“Nope,” I said.

But I honestly felt good about that.  My next big adventure was before me, and I still retained the most essential part of EvilCorp—my friend.

“Okay, well, when I heard you quit, I called a friend of mine over at that place Ed is always pissed about,” he said,” the one that is always stealing our clients.”

I’d tried to get a job over there BEFORE EvilCorp but didn’t have enough experience.  They never “stole” any clients.  The clients left after they realized EvilCorp had sold them an inferior product.

“Well, I told her about your presentation and sent her a copy,” Charlie said, “ I hope that is okay?”

“Sure.  Thanks?”  I said, a little confused.

“She got back to me quickly and said you were exactly what they were looking for, and they’d like to interview you tomorrow.  Are you cool with that?” he said.

“Sure.  Thanks, man.  I appreciate it.”

“No worries.  That’s what friends are for, right?”

“Yeah, I guess so.”

  “Oh, and you are buying me a margarita after.”

“Definitely.”

#

Author’s Notes:  I did write a short story in middle school (called junior high for me) called “The Darkness Scares Him Not…”. Hopefully, I will be able to include the picture I drew for it cause it did give me sentimental solid feelings.  I was sitting at my desk pondering a modern take on Pinnochio where the characters were female.  I won’t go into more cause I might write it yet.  But as I was considering this and taking notes, I just happened to be looking at my bookcase and saw the black construction paper poking out, and it occurred to me that my old story could be a fairy tale of sorts.  Then we talked a little about gothic style in class, and I just HAD to make it more gothic.  I had ideas of keeping it intact, but it was ten pages long and well…I wrote it when I was 13 or 14.  So, I included a heavily edited and trimmed version, but the first sentence is the same, and the premise and characters are the same.
